Caveat

I’d much rather be looking at individual losses.

Definitions

  • IBNYR = Incurred But Not Yet Reported
  • IBNER = Incurred But Not Enough Reported

The problem

  • Aggregate triangles are monolithic. We must introduce claim report triangles and infer (or guess) to split total reserves into IBNYR and IBNER.
  • Statements about claim emergence (IBNYR) and claim settlement (IBNR) follow after analysis.
  • Typical approach: project ultimate amounts and ultimate counts, then allocate incrementals from the one to the other. How? Meh.

The problem (2)

  • Multi-measure techniques like Berquist-Sherman or Adler-Kline focus on frequency/severity splits, not IBNYR/IBNER splits.
  • Frequency/severity splits tend to be concerned with those values at ultimate.

Example triangle

  • 100 policies per year for 10 years
  • Claim frequency ~ Poisson(5)
  • No of claim payments ~ Poisson(2)
  • Claims occur uniformly throughout the year
  • Report wait time ~ gamma, mean = 180 days, CV = 75%
  • Wait time between payments ~ gamma, mean = 45 days, CV = 300%
  • Payment ~ gamma, mean = 5,000, CV = 110%

Evaluation dates

Evaluation dates are an artificial construct which we impose on individual claim data. They’re arbitrary and have a lot more to do with financial reporting than the underlying stochastic process.

And we’re just going to leave that idea there and move one.

So, what does it all mean when estimating?

Four scenarios

  1. Claims are reported fast and they are paid (settled) fast -> Auto physical damage
  2. Claims are reported fast, but they are paid slowly -> Primary workers comp
  3. Claims are reported slowly, but they are paid fast -> Some forms of excess
  4. Claims are reported and paid slowly -> Mass tort

The simulation experiment

Create 100 individual claim simulations. Aggregate into monolithic and modular triangle(s).

  • Monolithic -> IBNER and IBNYR are combined
  • Modular -> Fit two triangles and then combine the results

Use standard chain ladder to estimate results.

What does it all mean?

  1. Slow payment (for this simulation!) has greater variance and bias.
  2. Chain ladder produces results that are biased high (consistent w/Stanard). Splitting our data doesn’t help us.

What does it all mean?

Actually, I couldn’t lose with the simulation.

  1. If there’s a big difference, it means we should split the data.
  2. If there isn’t, it means that we’ve got nothing to lose from switching methods.
